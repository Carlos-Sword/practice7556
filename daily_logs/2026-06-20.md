## Design Reasoning

The chassis was designed around the air system because the pneumatic tanks and pneumatic engine take up a large amount of space and strongly affect the robot’s weight distribution. By placing the tanks, engine, steering system, Motion 2350, and battery pack in the CAD model, we can better understand how the robot will fit together before manufacturing parts.

This helps prevent problems such as:

* Components not fitting after printing/building.
* Poor weight distribution.
* Hard-to-access electronics.
* Air tanks interfering with the drivetrain or steering.
* Steering system interference with the chassis.
* Battery placement making the robot unbalanced.

## Problems Found

The main challenge is fitting all systems into the chassis while keeping the robot compact and serviceable. The pneumatic system takes a lot of space, so the chassis must be designed around it instead of adding the air system later.

Important concerns:

* The air tanks need secure mounting.
* The pneumatic engine needs enough space to rotate and connect to the transmission.
* The steering system needs clearance to turn without hitting the chassis.
* The Motion 2350 and battery pack need to be accessible for wiring, testing, and repairs.
* The final layout must leave space for sensors and wiring.

## Changes Made

## Engineering Importance

This step is important because it connects multiple robot subsystems together:

* Mechanical: chassis, pneumatic engine, steering, air tanks.
* Electrical: Motion 2350, battery pack, wiring space.
* Pneumatic: tanks, engine, valve/regulator space.
* Future sensors: camera, encoders, and distance sensors.

Instead of designing each part separately, the robot is now being designed as one complete system. This should make the final robot more reliable and easier to assemble.
